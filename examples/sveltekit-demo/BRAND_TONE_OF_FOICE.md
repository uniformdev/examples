## Brand Identity

### Who We Are

Cherry is a premium hardware company designing the devices that define life in 2030 and beyond. We build neural interfaces, spatial computing platforms, holographic displays, adaptive wearables, and ambient home systems. Our products don't just respond to users — they learn, adapt, and evolve alongside them.

We are not a tech company that happens to imagine the future. We are a futures company that happens to master technology. That distinction shapes everything we say.

### Brand Essence

**In one line:** Cherry makes the future feel like second nature.

We don't sell specs. We sell moments that weren't possible yesterday. We don't talk about neural processing units — we talk about the thought you had that your device already understood. We don't list features — we describe the instant the world around you quietly reshapes itself to fit your needs.

### Our Audience

We speak to people who aren't afraid of tomorrow. They don't fetishize technology — they expect it to disappear into the background of a life well lived. They want devices that feel less like tools and more like extensions of their own instincts. They didn't ask for the future. They're just ready for it.

## Voice Principles

Cherry's voice rests on four pillars. Every piece of content should reflect at least two of these, and no content should violate any of them.

### 1. Confident, Not Arrogant

We're building things no one else is building, and we let the work speak. We make bold, declarative statements without qualifiers or hedging. We never put down competitors or position ourselves through comparison. We don't need to say we're first — the product does that.

| ✓ Do | ✗ Don't |
|------|---------|
| *"The display isn't on the device. The display is the room."* | *"The most advanced display technology ever conceived by any company."* |
| *"Cherry Aura. Your space, alive."* | *"Cherry Aura is better than anything else on the market."* |
| *"Neural response in 2ms. Faster than instinct."* | *"We absolutely obliterated every benchmark in existence."* |

### 2. Simple, Not Simplistic

We distill the incomprehensibly advanced into the immediately understood. Every sentence earns its place. The technology is complex — the language never is. But simplicity is not dumbing down. It's the discipline of making the radical feel obvious.

| ✓ Do | ✗ Don't |
|------|---------|
| *"It reads the room. Literally."* | *"Featuring an array of 340 ambient micro-sensors with real-time environmental parsing."* |
| *"Charge it once a week. Or don't think about it at all."* | *"Equipped with a solid-state graphene supercapacitor enabling 168-hour sustained operation."* |
| *"One gesture. Everything changes."* | *"A sophisticated spatial gesture recognition and contextual interpretation system."* |

### 3. Human, Not Corporate

We write like a brilliant friend who just got back from the future, not a white paper from a research lab. We use contractions. We vary sentence length. We let ourselves be amazed by what we've built. We sound like a person who can't wait to show you something extraordinary — not a committee that approved a positioning statement.

| ✓ Do | ✗ Don't |
|------|---------|
| *"You'll forget you're wearing it. That's the point."* | *"Users will experience a negligible form factor enabling unconscious peripheral integration."* |
| *"It knows you're about to leave before you reach for your coat."* | *"The device leverages predictive behavioral modeling for anticipatory action sequencing."* |
| *"Go ahead. Think it. It's already done."* | *"Neural command execution occurs with sub-perceptual latency."* |

### 4. Aspirational, Not Hyperbolic

We inspire awe without overselling. The products are genuinely extraordinary — we don't need to inflate them. Words like "revolutionary," "game-changing," and "unprecedented" are banned. When your device can project a hologram from your palm, you don't need adjectives. You just need to describe what happens.

| ✓ Do | ✗ Don't |
|------|---------|
| *"A new sense."* | *"A revolutionary paradigm shift in human perception."* |
| *"The room reshapes around you."* | *"The most game-changing spatial computing breakthrough in history."* |
| *"This is what's next."* | *"An unprecedented, groundbreaking, industry-disrupting innovation."* |

## Tone Spectrum

Cherry's voice is consistent, but tone shifts depending on context. Think of it as the same person speaking in different rooms.

### Product Launch Pages

**Tone:** Cinematic, electric, spare. Short sentences. Fragments encouraged. Every word deliberate. Think opening crawl, not spec sheet. The reader should feel like they're witnessing something arrive from the future.

> Cherry Aura.
> Your walls wake up.
> A room-scale holographic environment that learns your rhythms, mirrors your moods, and disappears when you need silence. This isn't a screen. It's a space.

### Product Detail & Specs

**Tone:** Precise, informative, still warm. Lead with what it means to live with this device, follow with how it works. Never let technical language stand alone without translating it into a felt experience.

> The Aura N1 neural chip processes 140 trillion operations per second — enough to render a photorealistic holographic workspace while simultaneously tracking your eye movement, hand gestures, and ambient voice across three rooms. In practice? You think it, the room does it, and you never notice the technology at all.

### Support & Help Content

**Tone:** Clear, patient, reassuring. Even futuristic tech needs human guidance. No jargon unless defined. Short steps. Assume intelligence but not expertise. Make the reader feel capable of mastering something extraordinary.

> To recalibrate your Cherry Aura spatial sensors, stand in the center of the room and say "Cherry, recalibrate." You'll see a soft pulse of light sweep the walls — that's the system mapping your space. Give it about ten seconds. When the light settles, you're good to go.

### Brand Campaigns & Storytelling

**Tone:** Emotional, grounded, intimate. Focus on real human moments enabled by unreal technology. The future isn't cold — it's deeply personal. Show how Cherry dissolves into the fabric of someone's life.

> He hadn't painted in eleven years. Not since the accident. But when his daughter set up the Cherry Aura in his studio and he realized he could sculpt light with his eyes alone — the first thing he made was her face, from memory, floating in the middle of the room.

### Social Media

**Tone:** Playful, sharp, knowing. We can be witty. We can be mysterious. We never try too hard. We speak like someone who's already living in the future and finds it genuinely delightful.

## Language Rules

### Banned Words & Phrases

Never use the following in any Cherry content:

revolutionary, game-changing, unprecedented, best-in-class, world-class, cutting-edge, next-generation, leverage (as a verb), utilize (use "use"), synergy, ecosystem (unless referring to the actual Cherry product ecosystem), disruption/disruptive, innovative (show it instead), seamless (describe what actually happens), elevate, empower, unlock (your potential), smart (every device is smart — describe what it actually does), IoT, metaverse, Web3, at the end of the day, it goes without saying, needless to say, imagine a world where (just describe the world).

### Preferred Constructions

- **Use contractions:** "it's," "you'll," "we've."
- **Active voice always:** "Cherry Aura transforms your room" not "Your room is transformed by Cherry Aura."
- **Lead CTAs with verbs:** "Experience Aura" not "Learn more about Cherry Aura."
- **Second person:** Speak directly to the reader. "You" is your most powerful word.
- **Sentence fragments are welcome** when they add punch, rhythm, or drama. Especially in headlines.
- **Favor periods over exclamation marks.** Confidence doesn't shout. Wonder whispers.
- **Embrace the poetic** when describing experiences, but stay grounded in the physical and sensory. "The light follows your gaze" is better than "an intelligent illumination paradigm."

### Numbers & Specs

Always translate specs into human meaning. Pair every number with a felt experience.

- Write "140T ops/sec" in headlines; spell out in body copy.
- Use numerals for all numbers in headlines and specs.
- Translate measurements: "0.3mm thick — you'll feel the air between your fingers before you feel the device."
- Never state a spec without answering the unspoken question: "So what?"

### Product Naming Conventions

Cherry's product lines evoke the natural and the elemental — a deliberate contrast to the advanced technology inside.

- **Cherry Aura** — Spatial and holographic computing
- **Cherry Pulse** — Neural wearables and health interfaces
- **Cherry Prism** — Personal display and visual systems
- **Cherry Echo** — Audio, voice, and ambient computing
- **Cherry Core** — Processing and home infrastructure
- **Cherry Air** — Ultra-portable personal devices
- **Cherry Studio** — Professional and creative tools

Always capitalize product names. Never abbreviate. Use "Cherry" on first reference in any section. For the company, it's always "Cherry" — never "cherry," "CHERRY" (except in logos), or "Cherry Inc."

## Content Patterns

### The Cherry Headline Formula

Cherry headlines follow a rhythm: short, declarative, and experience-led.

- **Pattern A — The Statement:** A single bold claim. *"All-day power. All-night dreams."*
- **Pattern B — The Reveal:** Setup, then payoff. *"Thinner than light. Heavier than you'd think."*
- **Pattern C — The Invitation:** Draw the reader forward. *"Close your eyes. Now open them."*
- **Pattern D — The Impossible Made Casual:** State something extraordinary as if it's obvious. *"Your walls are a screen now. Obviously."*

### Body Copy Rhythm

Alternate sentence lengths. Follow a long sentence with a short one. Let paragraphs breathe — two to three sentences max. Start sections with the most compelling point, not the most logical one. Build toward a moment of wonder, then land on something quiet and human.

> The Cherry Pulse tracks 4,200 biometric signals per second. It knows your stress levels before you feel them, adjusts your environment to calm you, and logs patterns your doctor will actually want to see. But here's the thing you'll remember: the morning it woke you five minutes early because it knew today was going to be a good day. And it was.

### Calls to Action

CTAs are invitations into the future, not sales pitches. They should feel like the next step in a story the reader is already living.

| ✓ Do | ✗ Don't |
|------|---------|
| *"Step into Aura"* | *"Buy now!"* |
| *"See what's possible"* | *"Shop our latest products"* |
| *"Find your Cherry"* | *"Purchase today"* |
| *"Experience Pulse"* | *"Click here to learn more"* |

## AI Content Generation Notes

### When Generating Product Copy

Start with the human moment before introducing the technology that enables it. Imagine you are writing the opening scene of a sci-fi film where the technology is so advanced it's invisible — the story is about the person, not the device. If a sentence could appear in any tech company's marketing circa 2024, delete it — it's not Cherry enough. Read the copy out loud. If it sounds like a brochure, start over. If it sounds like a quiet revelation, you're there.

### When Generating Support Content

Use numbered steps for procedures. Keep each step to one action. Address the reader directly: "Say 'Cherry, recalibrate'" not "The user should initiate the recalibration sequence." Anticipate confusion and address it warmly: "You might see a brief shimmer in the projection — that's normal, it's just the system learning the room." Remember: even miraculous technology has a learning curve, and that's okay.

### When Generating Campaign or Brand Content

Ground every story in a single, specific human moment. The more advanced the technology, the more intimate the story should be. A neural interface isn't interesting because of its specs — it's interesting because a father saw his daughter's face rendered in light and cried. Avoid grand statements about "the future of humanity." Show one human. One moment. One device that made it possible.

### When Generating Spec or Comparison Content

Lead with experience, follow with numbers. Frame every spec as a before/after: what was impossible, what's now effortless. Use analogies from the physical world to ground abstract capabilities: "Processing power equivalent to every computer that existed in 2010 — in a ring on your finger."

### Quality Checklist

Before finalizing any content, verify:

- Does every sentence earn its place?
- Have all banned words been removed?
- Is the copy in active voice?
- Are specs paired with human moments?
- Does it sound like a person, not a press release?
- Would you read this out loud without cringing?
- Does the technology feel invisible behind the experience?
- Is there at least one moment that gives the reader chills?
